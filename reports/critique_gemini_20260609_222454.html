3. Model Limitations

  • ARIMA:
    • Assumes underlying stationarity (or transformable to it), which is rarely perfectly true for financial time series and can break down under structural changes.
    • Struggles with non-linear relationships and regime shifts; it will simply extrapolate past patterns, even if those patterns are breaking down.
    • Does not incorporate exogenous variables or fundamental catalysts, making its forecasts purely a reflection of price history.
  • Prophet:
    • Designed for time series with strong seasonal components, which equity prices often lack in a consistent, predictable manner over short horizons.
    • Can be overly aggressive in detecting and fitting trends and seasonality, potentially leading to overfitting noise in financial data.
    • Also a purely historical price-based model, blind to fundamental news or external events.
  • Ensemble Forecast (Average): Averaging two models that "diverge significantly" does not mitigate the underlying uncertainty or improve predictive power; it merely smooths out conflicting signals without resolving their root causes. A "neutral" agreement still indicates substantial unresolved uncertainty.
  • "Bullish Probability" Score:
    • Not a Probability: This is a weighted heuristic score, not a statistically calibrated probability. It is based on a set of rules (e.g., RSI > 50, MACD positive) and indicator positions, not on empirical frequencies or a statistically significant model trained to predict actual outcomes.
    • Lack of Validation: To be considered a probability, it would require rigorous backtesting across thousands of similar historical scenarios, demonstrating that when the score was, for example, 60%, the stock actually moved up X% of the time. This validation is entirely absent.
    • No Confidence Intervals: A true probability would come with confidence intervals, indicating the range of potential outcomes. This score provides a single, point estimate without any measure of its own uncertainty.
    • Heuristic-Driven: It merely quantifies indicator alignment; it does not predict earnings catalysts or fundamental shifts that truly drive price.
