3. Model Limitations

The reliance on ARIMA, Prophet, and a rule-based "bullish probability" score presents severe limitations, especially for short-term forecasts in dynamic markets.

  • ARIMA & Prophet - Inherent Weaknesses:
    • Non-Stationarity & Regime Changes: Both ARIMA and Prophet struggle significantly when underlying market dynamics shift rapidly, failing to adequately account for "regime changes" (e.g., sudden policy shifts, new economic cycles, geopolitical shocks) which invalidate past statistical relationships. They are built on the assumption that past patterns will continue, a dangerous premise in volatile markets.
    • Fat Tails & Extreme Events: These models are generally designed for normal distributions and do not adequately capture "fat tail" events – low-probability, high-impact occurrences (e.g., flash crashes, unexpected market rallies/sell-offs due to news). The "simple average" ensemble does nothing to mitigate this.
    • Lack of Causal Factors: Neither model can incorporate fundamental news, earnings catalysts, competitive developments, or macro-economic data points directly. They only react to the price action after these events, making them inherently lagging indicators for significant shifts. Price history alone does not predict earnings beats or new product launches.
  • Rule-Based "Bullish Probability" Score - Not a Probability:
    • The "rule-based technical probability" (e.g., 42% bullish for AAPL) is a misnomer. It is a heuristic, an arbitrary weighting of technical indicators (RSI, MACD, SMA stack, Bollinger %B, ADX, forecast direction/agreement) that produces a score, not a statistically calibrated probability.
    • Lack of Validation: The report provides no evidence that this score represents a true probability. To be a statistically valid probability, it would require:
      • Rigorous Backtesting: Demonstrating consistent out-of-sample predictive power over diverse market conditions.
      • Statistical Significance: Proving the score's predictive ability is not due to random chance.
      • Calibration: Showing that when the model assigns, say, a 60% bullish probability, the asset actually moves higher 60% of the time.
      • Defined Alpha: Showing that acting on these "probabilities" generates consistent returns above a benchmark.
    • Without such validation, it is merely an opinion generated by a formula, not a "probability," and presenting it as such is an act of overconfidence. The method for deriving weights for its components is also entirely opaque.
